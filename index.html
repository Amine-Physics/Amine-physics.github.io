Smooth Manifolds · §1

C∞(M, ℝ) is Infinite Dimensional

Let \(M\) be a smooth manifold of dimension \(n \geq 1\). We show that the vector space \(C^\infty(M, \mathbb{R})\) of smooth real-valued functions on \(M\) is infinite dimensional.

Theorem 1.1

Let \(M\) be a smooth manifold with \(\dim M \geq 1\). Then \(C^\infty(M,\mathbb{R})\) is infinite dimensional as a real vector space.

The key ingredient is the existence of smooth bump functions, which we recall first.

Lemma 1.2 (Smooth Bump Functions)

For any point \(p \in M\) and any open neighbourhood \(U \ni p\), there exists a smooth function \(\varphi \in C^\infty(M,\mathbb{R})\) such that \(\varphi(p) > 0\) and \(\operatorname{supp}(\varphi) \subset U\).

Proof of Theorem 1.1

We produce an infinite linearly independent set in \(C^\infty(M,\mathbb{R})\). Since \(M\) is a manifold of dimension \(n \geq 1\), it contains infinitely many points. Choose a sequence of distinct points \(\{p_k\}_{k=1}^\infty \subset M\).

Since \(M\) is a Hausdorff space, for each \(k\) we can find pairwise disjoint open sets \(U_k\) with \(p_k \in U_k\). By Lemma 1.2, for each \(k\) there exists \(\varphi_k \in C^\infty(M,\mathbb{R})\) with \(\varphi_k(p_k) > 0\) and \(\operatorname{supp}(\varphi_k) \subset U_k\).

We claim \(\{\varphi_k\}_{k \geq 1}\) is linearly independent. Suppose \(\sum_{k=1}^N c_k \varphi_k = 0\) for some \(c_k \in \mathbb{R}\). Evaluating at \(p_j\) for a fixed \(j \leq N\): \[ 0 = \sum_{k=1}^N c_k \varphi_k(p_j) = c_j \varphi_j(p_j), \] where we used that \(\operatorname{supp}(\varphi_k) \subset U_k\) and \(p_j \notin U_k\) for \(k \neq j\), so \(\varphi_k(p_j) = 0\). Since \(\varphi_j(p_j) > 0\), we conclude \(c_j = 0\). As \(j\) was arbitrary, all coefficients vanish, confirming linear independence.

Since \(C^\infty(M,\mathbb{R})\) contains an infinite linearly independent set, it is infinite dimensional. \(\square\)

□
Remark

The same argument shows that for any non-discrete topological space admitting bump functions — in particular any smooth manifold — the algebra of smooth functions is infinite dimensional. The Hausdorff property is essential: it guarantees the existence of the disjoint neighbourhoods \(U_k\).

Smooth Manifolds · §2

Retracts of Hausdorff Spaces are Closed

Definition 2.1 (Retract)

Let \(X\) be a topological space and \(A \subset X\) a subspace. We call \(A\) a retract of \(X\) if there exists a continuous map \(r : X \to A\) such that \(r|_A = \mathrm{id}_A\), i.e.\ \(r(a) = a\) for all \(a \in A\). The map \(r\) is called a retraction.

Theorem 2.2

If \(X\) is a Hausdorff space and \(A \subset X\) is a retract of \(X\), then \(A\) is closed in \(X\).

Proof

Let \(r : X \to A \subset X\) be a retraction. Consider the two continuous maps \[ r,\, \iota \circ r : X \rightrightarrows X, \] where \(\iota : A \hookrightarrow X\) is the inclusion, so \(\iota \circ r = r\) viewed as a map into \(X\). More usefully, consider \(r\) and \(\mathrm{id}_X\).

We show \(A\) is closed by showing its complement is open, or equivalently by showing \(A\) equals the equaliser of two continuous maps. Observe that \[ A = \{ x \in X : r(x) = x \} = \mathrm{Eq}(r,\, \mathrm{id}_X). \] Indeed, if \(x \in A\) then \(r(x) = x\) by the retraction property, so \(x \in \mathrm{Eq}(r, \mathrm{id}_X)\). Conversely if \(r(x) = x\) then \(x = r(x) \in A\) since \(r\) maps into \(A\).

Since \(X\) is Hausdorff, for any two distinct points \(x, y \in X\) there exist disjoint open sets separating them. This implies that the diagonal \(\Delta_X \subset X \times X\) is closed. Equivalently, the equaliser of any two continuous maps into a Hausdorff space is closed. More precisely, the map \[ \phi : X \to X \times X, \quad x \mapsto (r(x),\, x) \] is continuous, and \[ A = \mathrm{Eq}(r,\mathrm{id}_X) = \phi^{-1}(\Delta_X). \] Since \(\Delta_X\) is closed in \(X \times X\) (by Hausdorffness of \(X\)) and \(\phi\) is continuous, the preimage \(\phi^{-1}(\Delta_X) = A\) is closed in \(X\). \(\square\)

□
Remark

The Hausdorff condition is necessary. In a non-Hausdorff space the diagonal need not be closed, and retracts need not be closed. For smooth manifolds this is never an issue since manifolds are by definition Hausdorff (and second countable).

Smooth Manifolds · §3

Partitions of Unity

Definition 3.1

A partition of unity subordinate to an open cover \(\{U_\alpha\}\) of \(M\) is a collection of smooth functions \(\{\psi_\alpha\} \subset C^\infty(M,[0,1])\) such that: (i) \(\operatorname{supp}(\psi_\alpha) \subset U_\alpha\) for each \(\alpha\), (ii) the supports are locally finite, and (iii) \(\sum_\alpha \psi_\alpha \equiv 1\) on \(M\).

Theorem 3.2

Every smooth manifold admits a partition of unity subordinate to any open cover.

Heat Kernel Methods · §4

The Heat Kernel

Let \((M, g)\) be a closed Riemannian manifold and \(\Delta_g\) the Laplace–Beltrami operator acting on \(C^\infty(M)\). The heat equation on \(M\) is \[ \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \Delta_g\right) u = 0, \quad u : M \times (0,\infty) \to \mathbb{R}. \]

Definition 4.1 (Heat Kernel)

The heat kernel \(K_t(x,y)\) is the smooth function \(K : M \times M \times (0,\infty) \to \mathbb{R}\) satisfying:

(i) \(\left(\partial_t + \Delta_x\right) K_t(x,y) = 0\) for all \(y\),

(ii) \(\lim_{t \to 0^+} K_t(x,\cdot) = \delta_x\) in the sense of distributions,

(iii) \(K_t(x,y) = K_t(y,x)\) (symmetry).

The solution to the heat equation with initial data \(f \in C^\infty(M)\) is then given by \[ u(x,t) = \int_M K_t(x,y)\, f(y)\, \mathrm{d}\mathrm{vol}_g(y). \]

Spectral Interpretation

Since \(M\) is closed, \(\Delta_g\) has a discrete spectrum \(0 = \lambda_0 \leq \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \to \infty\) with an orthonormal basis of eigenfunctions \(\{\phi_k\}\). The heat kernel has the spectral expansion \[ K_t(x,y) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty e^{-\lambda_k t}\, \phi_k(x)\, \phi_k(y), \] convergent in \(C^\infty\) for \(t > 0\).

Heat Kernel Methods · §5

Asymptotic Expansion

One of the most important results in geometric analysis is that the heat kernel admits an asymptotic expansion as \(t \to 0^+\) whose coefficients encode geometric invariants of \((M,g)\).

Theorem 5.1 (Heat Kernel Asymptotics)

As \(t \to 0^+\), the trace of the heat kernel has the asymptotic expansion \[ \mathrm{Tr}(e^{-t\Delta_g}) = \int_M K_t(x,x)\, \mathrm{d}\mathrm{vol}_g \sim (4\pi t)^{-n/2} \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k(M)\, t^k, \] where \(n = \dim M\) and the heat invariants \(a_k(M)\) are integrals of local geometric quantities: \[ a_0 = \mathrm{Vol}(M), \quad a_1 = \frac{1}{6}\int_M R\, \mathrm{d}\mathrm{vol}_g, \] with \(R\) the scalar curvature, and higher \(a_k\) involving curvature polynomials of increasing order.

Application: Weyl's Law

From the \(t \to 0^+\) asymptotics one recovers Weyl's law for the eigenvalue counting function: \[ N(\lambda) := \#\{k : \lambda_k \leq \lambda\} \sim \frac{\omega_n \cdot \mathrm{Vol}(M)}{(2\pi)^n}\, \lambda^{n/2} \quad \text{as } \lambda \to \infty, \] where \(\omega_n\) is the volume of the unit ball in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). This shows that the geometry of \(M\) is encoded in the spectrum of \(\Delta_g\) — one can "hear the volume of the drum."

Note — Proof Strategy

The proof constructs an approximate heat kernel (parametrix) \(\tilde{K}_t(x,y)\) locally using the Euclidean heat kernel \((4\pi t)^{-n/2} e^{-|x-y|^2/4t}\) corrected by the local geometry, then shows the error is negligible as \(t \to 0^+\). Full details to be added.
