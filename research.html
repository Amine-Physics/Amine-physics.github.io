Master Thesis

In this thesis, I investigated gravitational time delay in the framework of Hassan--Rosen bimetric gravity, a ghost-free extension of General Relativity featuring both a massless and a massive spin-2 field. The goal was to derive observable consequences of the theory and confront them with high-precision experimental data. The core of the work consisted in reformulating gravitational interactions as a scattering problem in the weak-field limit. Considering the interaction of scalar particles mediated by both massless and massive gravitons, I computed the scattering amplitude in the eikonal regime, where the center-of-mass energy dominates over momentum transfer. In this regime, the scattering matrix exponentiates, allowing the extraction of the eikonal phase, from which the classical gravitational time delay is obtained via a stationary phase approximation. This approach provides a direct connection between quantum field theoretic quantities and classical gravitational observables. The resulting time delay contains the standard contribution from General Relativity, together with corrections arising from the massive spin-2 mode of the theory. To test the physical viability of the model, I extended the formalism to describe the propagation of light in the gravitational field of the Sun, modeling the system as the interaction between a massless and a massive scalar particle. Using observational constraints from the Cassini space mission on the post-Newtonian parameter $\gamma$, I derived bounds on the parameter space of the theory, constraining both the coupling strength and the mass scale of the massive graviton. Finally, I explored a novel application of the Wigner--Smith time delay operator to gravitational scattering in bimetric gravity. I showed that, at leading order, the operator reproduces the eikonal result, and investigated higher-order contributions involving multi-particle intermediate states, highlighting both the potential and the limitations of this approach beyond the eikonal approximation. Overall, this work establishes a pipeline connecting effective field theory descriptions of gravity to experimentally measurable quantities, illustrating how classical gravitational phenomena can emerge from scattering amplitudes.

Current Projects

Research paper: Eikonal Time Delay and Post-Newtonian Constraints in Bimetric Gravity

In this work, I extend the investigation of time delay effects in bimetric gravity. In my Master’s thesis, the photon was modeled as a spin-0 particle, justified by the fact that spin-dependent corrections are subleading in the classical limit. Here, I refine this analysis by explicitly incorporating the photon’s spin. The time delay is computed using stationary phase methods, and constraints on the parameter space of the theory are derived from solar system time delay measurements.
